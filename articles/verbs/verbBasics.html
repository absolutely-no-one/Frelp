Verb basics

Main takeaways

  • Verbs can end in er, ir, or re
  • All verbs must be conjugated -- like how English verb "to be" turns into "I am"
  • Most verbs follow a conjugation pattern based on their ending
    • Some verbs do not follow a pattern, they are known as "irregular verbs"

What are verbs & how do they function?


Verbs are action words. In English, some examples could include "I run" or "you eat." They signal the task that someone is doing - they can also signify a state of being, such as in the sentence "he is hungry."

The way that verbs work is very interesting. For each subject (I, you, he/she/it, we, you all, they), the verb must be changed based on the subject. Take the verb "to be" for instance. You say "I am," but "you are." Saying "I are" just sounds wrong. This is called conjugation, and applies across all tenses (such as "I was" meaning "I am, but am no longer anymore." You would never say "I am" to mean that you used to be), but for now it is easier just to focus on the present tense.


Like English, French has conjugation, but it is much more noticable. Look at the table below:

English - to like French - aimer
I like J'aime
You like Tu aimes
He/she/one likes Il/elle/on aime
We like Nous aimons
You all like Vous aimez
They like Ils/elles aiment

As you can see, in English there are only two versions of the verb "to like" in the present tense. But in French, nearly all of the conjugations are different!


How do I remember them?

Many verbs follow a conjugation pattern based on their ending. Aimer, as you can see, ends in "er" and is a 'regular' -er verb, meaning it follows a set pattern. To conjugate regular -er verbs, you would simply drop the infinitive (-er in this case) and add the following endings:

Subject Ending
Je -e
Tu -es
Il/elle/on -e
Nous -ons
Vous -ez
Ils/elles -ent

(You may be wondering why its "j'aime" instead of "je aime") It might take some getting used to, but after a while conjugating becomes second nature. The other conjugations for regular verbs can be found here.
